Principles and Scalability

Modern computer vision pipelines depend on powerful pretrained backbones (vision encoders, diffusion models, and vision–language models). The decisive gains for real-world use often come after pretraining—through post-training such as fine-tuning, alignment, test-time adaptation, model editing/unlearning, and reasoning-oriented procedures. This Special Issue invites advances that improve effectiveness, efficiency, and adaptability across recognition and generation tasks.

About the Special Issue

We invite innovative research on post-training for computer vision systems. Submissions may address full or parameter-efficient fine-tuning, instruction and preference alignment, inference-time adaptation, model editing and unlearning, and techniques that enhance the reliability and scalability of models across diverse tasks (e.g., image classification/generation, video understanding).

Aim & Scope

We encourage submissions across visual understanding and generation, including (but not limited to): image classification, object detection, instance/semantic/panoptic segmentation, keypoint/pose estimation, action recognition, visual grounding and VQA, tracking, retrieval, captioning, image/video generation and editing, 3D perception and reconstruction, and embodied/robotic perception.

Potential Topics of Interest

  • Parameter-efficient adaptation: adapters/LoRA, visual prompt tuning, soft/hard prompts, hypernetworks, and rank/structure selection for rapid specialization under tight compute and memory budgets.
  • Instruction & preference tuning for vision/VLMs: building and using instruction datasets; pairwise/listwise preference learning (RL-free or RL-based objectives); aligning models to human or task-specific criteria for recognition, localization, captioning, and generation.
  • Inference-time & test-time adaptation: on-the-fly strategies such as entropy/objective-driven updates, batch-norm/feature alignment, online/episodic adaptation, retrieval-augmented inference, self-consistency, verifier/rescorer pipelines, and dynamic prompt optimization.
  • Multi-modality, 3D, and temporal modeling: post-training across image–text–audio; video and action understanding; point clouds and meshes; 3D reconstruction; and cross-modal grounding.

Ethical Responsibilities of Authors

IJCV is committed to upholding the integrity of the scientific record and follows the COPE guidelines on dealing with potential misconduct. Authors should adhere to good scientific practice, including (non-exhaustively):

  • Do not submit to more than one journal simultaneously.
  • Submit original work; clearly disclose any reuse or expansion of prior work to avoid text recycling.
  • Avoid salami-slicing (fragmenting a single study into multiple submissions).
  • Concurrent or secondary publication is sometimes justifiable if specific conditions are met (e.g., translations with clarity on audience).
  • Present results clearly and honestly without fabrication, falsification, or inappropriate manipulation (including image-based manipulation).
  • Follow discipline-specific rules for data acquisition, selection, and processing.
  • Acknowledge others’ work properly; use quotation marks for verbatim text and secure permissions for copyrighted material.

IJCV may screen submissions for plagiarism. For details, see the IJCV Submission Guidelines.
