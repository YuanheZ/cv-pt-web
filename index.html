Principles and Scalability of Post‑Training

Modern vision systems depend on enormous pre‑trained backbones, but true performance gains come after pre‑training through careful post‑training. This special issue explores innovative methods to adapt vision and vision–language models at scale, spanning fine‑tuning, instruction alignment, inference‑time adaptation and more.

Introduction & Scope

Modern computer vision systems increasingly rely on large pretrained backbones such as vision encoders, diffusion models and vision–language models. The decisive gains for real‑world use, however, often arise after pre‑training — through post‑training procedures like fine‑tuning (full or parameter‑efficient), instruction/preference alignment, test‑time adaptation, model editing/unlearning and reasoning‑oriented procedures. The aim of this special issue is to invite innovative research papers that address the challenges of post‑training and propose novel techniques that improve effectiveness, efficiency and adaptability across recognition and generation tasks.

We encourage submissions that cover a broad range of visual understanding and generation tasks, including but not limited to image classification, object detection, instance/semantic/panoptic segmentation, keypoint/pose estimation, action recognition, visual grounding and visual question answering (VQA), tracking, retrieval, captioning, image/video generation and editing, 3D perception and reconstruction, and embodied or robotic perception.

Topics of Interest

Potential topics of interest include, but are not limited to:

  • Parameter‑efficient adaptation, such as methods for adapters/LoRA, visual prompt tuning, soft/hard prompts, hypernetworks and rank/structure selection that enable rapid specialisation under tight compute and memory budgets.
  • Instruction and preference tuning for vision and vision–language models, including techniques for building and using instruction datasets, pairwise or listwise preference learning (e.g., RL‑free or RL‑based objectives), and aligning models to human or task‑specific criteria for recognition, localisation, captioning and generation.
  • Inference‑time and test‑time adaptation, covering strategies that adapt on the fly — entropy or objective‑driven updates, batch‑norm or feature alignment, online/episodic adaptation, retrieval‑augmented inference, self‑consistency, verifier/rescorer pipelines and dynamic prompt optimisation.
  • Multi‑modality, 3D and temporal modelling, encompassing post‑training across image–text–audio modalities, video and action understanding, point clouds and meshes, 3D reconstruction and cross‑modal grounding.

Submission Guidelines & Resources

Manuscripts should present original, unpublished work relevant to one of the topics of this special issue. Submissions must be prepared according to the IJCV author guidelines and should not be under review at another journal or conference during the evaluation process.

Please submit via the IJCV Editorial Manager and choose “SI: Fine‑Tuning in Modern Machine Learning” from the article type dropdown. Each submission will be reviewed by at least two independent reviewers and evaluated for relevance, significance, technical quality and clarity of presentation.

Ethical Responsibilities

This journal is committed to upholding the integrity of the scientific record. Authors should follow good scientific practices to ensure trust in the publication process and the professionalism of scientific authorship. Key points include:

  • The manuscript should not be submitted to more than one journal for simultaneous consideration.
  • The submitted work should be original and should not have been published elsewhere in any form or language, unless the new work is an expansion of previous work; authors should provide transparency on reuse of material to avoid concerns about self‑plagiarism.
  • A single study should not be split up into several parts to increase the quantity of submissions (“salami publishing”).
  • Results should be presented clearly, honestly and without fabrication, falsification or inappropriate data manipulation (including image manipulation). Discipline‑specific rules for acquiring, selecting and processing data should be followed.
  • Authors must acknowledge the work of others appropriately and avoid presenting others’ data, text or theories as their own. Proper citations and permissions for copyrighted material are required.
